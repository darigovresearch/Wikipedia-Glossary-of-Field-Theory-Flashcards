"Field (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"<p>A <b>field</b> is a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring"" title=""Commutative ring"">commutative ring</a> (<i>F</i>,+,*) in which 0≠1 and every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse.  In a field we thus can perform the operations addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division.
</p><p>The non-zero elements of a field <i>F</i> form an <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group"" title=""Abelian group"">abelian group</a> under multiplication; this group is typically denoted by <i>F</i><sup>×</sup>;
</p><p>The <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring"" title=""Polynomial ring"">ring of polynomials</a> in the variable <i>x</i> with coefficients in <i>F</i> is denoted by <i>F</i>[<i>x</i>].
</p>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra)"" title=""Characteristic (algebra)"">Characteristic</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
The <i>characteristic</i> of the field <i>F</i> is the smallest positive <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer"" title=""Integer"">integer</a> <i>n</i> such that <i>n</i>·1 = 0; here <i>n</i>·1 stands for <i>n</i> summands 1 + 1 + 1 + ... + 1. If no such  <i>n</i> exists, we say the characteristic is zero. Every non-zero characteristic is a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number"" title=""Prime number"">prime number</a>. For example, the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number"" title=""Rational number"">rational numbers</a>, the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number"" title=""Real number"">real numbers</a> and the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_numbers"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""P-adic numbers""><i>p</i>-adic numbers</a> have characteristic 0, while the finite field <b>Z</b><sub><i>p</i></sub> where <i>p</i> is prime has characteristic <i>p</i>."
"<b>Subfield</b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A <i>subfield</i> of a field <i>F</i> is a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset"" title=""Subset"">subset</a> of <i>F</i> which is closed under the field operation + and * of <i>F</i> and which, with these operations, forms itself a field."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_field"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Prime field"">Prime field</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
The <i>prime field</i> of the field <i>F</i> is the unique smallest subfield of <i>F</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension"" title=""Field extension"">Extension field</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
If <i>F</i> is a subfield of <i>E</i> then <i>E</i> is an <i>extension field</i> of <i>F</i>. We then also say that <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> is a <i>field extension</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_a_field_extension"" title=""Degree of a field extension"">Degree of an extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
Given an extension <i>E</i>/<i>F</i>, the field <i>E</i> can be considered as a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space"" title=""Vector space"">vector space</a> over the field <i>F</i>, and the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space)"" title=""Dimension (vector space)"">dimension</a> of this vector space is the <i>degree</i> of the extension, denoted by [<i>E</i>&#160;: <i>F</i>]."
"<b>Finite extension</b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A <i>finite extension</i> is a field extension whose degree is finite."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_extension"" title=""Algebraic extension"">Algebraic extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
If an element α of an extension field <i>E</i> over <i>F</i> is the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root"" title=""Root"">root</a> of a non-zero polynomial in <i>F</i>[<i>x</i>], then α is <i>algebraic</i> over <i>F</i>.  If every element of <i>E</i> is algebraic over <i>F</i>, then <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> is an <i>algebraic extension</i>."
"<b>Generating set</b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
Given a field extension <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> and a subset <i>S</i> of <i>E</i>, we write <i>F</i>(<i>S</i>) for the smallest subfield of <i>E</i> that contains both <i>F</i> and <i>S</i>. It consists of all the elements of <i>E</i> that can be obtained by repeatedly using the operations +,−,*,/ on the elements of <i>F</i> and <i>S</i>. If <i>E</i> = <i>F</i>(<i>S</i>) we say that <i>E</i> is generated by <i>S</i> over <i>F</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_element_(field_theory)"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Primitive element (field theory)"">Primitive element</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An element α of an extension field <i>E</i> over a field <i>F</i> is called a <i>primitive element</i> if <i>E</i>=<i>F</i>(α), the smallest extension field containing α. Such an extension is called a <b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_extension"" title=""Simple extension"">simple extension</a></b>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Splitting_field"" title=""Splitting field"">Splitting field</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A field extension generated by the complete factorisation of a polynomial."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_extension"" title=""Normal extension"">Normal extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A field extension generated by the complete factorisation of a set of polynomials."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_extension"" title=""Separable extension"">Separable extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension generated by roots of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_polynomial"" title=""Separable polynomial"">separable polynomials</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_field"" title=""Perfect field"">Perfect field</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A field such that every finite extension is separable. All fields of characteristic zero, and all finite fields, are perfect."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Imperfect_degree&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1"" class=""new"" title=""Imperfect degree (page does not exist)"">Imperfect degree</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
Let <i>F</i> be a field of characteristic <i>p</i>&gt;0; then <i>F</i><sup><i>p</i></sup> is a subfield. The degree [<i>F</i>:<i>F</i><sup><i>p</i></sup>] is called the <i>imperfect degree</i> of <i>F</i>. The field <i>F</i> is perfect if and only if its imperfect degree is <i>1</i>. For example, if <i>F</i> is a function field of <i>n</i> variables over a finite field of characteristic <i>p</i>&gt;0, then its imperfect degree is <i>p</i><sup>n</sup>.<sup id=""cite_ref-FJ45_1-0"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-FJ45-1"">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field"" title=""Algebraically closed field"">Algebraically closed field</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A field <i>F</i> is <i>algebraically closed</i> if every polynomial in <i>F</i>[<i>x</i>] has a root in <i>F</i>; equivalently: every polynomial in <i>F</i>[<i>x</i>] is a product of linear factors."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_closure"" title=""Algebraic closure"">Algebraic closure</a></b> (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An <i>algebraic closure</i> of a field <i>F</i> is an algebraic extension of <i>F</i> which is algebraically closed. Every field has an algebraic closure, and it is unique up to an isomorphism that fixes <i>F</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_element"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Transcendental element"">Transcendental</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
Those elements of an extension field of <i>F</i> that are not algebraic over <i>F</i> are <i>transcendental</i> over <i>F</i>."
"<b>Algebraically independent elements</b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
Elements of an extension field of <i>F</i> are <i>algebraically independent</i> over <i>F</i> if they don't satisfy any non-zero polynomial equation with coefficients in <i>F</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendence_degree"" title=""Transcendence degree"">Transcendence degree</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
The number of algebraically independent transcendental elements in a field extension. It is used to define the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety"" title=""Dimension of an algebraic variety"">dimension of an algebraic variety</a>."
"<b>Field homomorphism</b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A <i>field homomorphism</i> between two fields <i>E</i> and <i>F</i> is a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)"" title=""Function (mathematics)"">function</a>
<dl><dd><i>f</i>&#160;: <i>E</i> → <i>F</i></dd></dl></dd>
<dd>such that, for all <i>x</i>, <i>y</i> in <i>E</i>,
<dl><dd><i>f</i>(<i>x</i> + <i>y</i>) = <i>f</i>(<i>x</i>) + <i>f</i>(<i>y</i>)</dd>
<dd><i>f</i>(<i>xy</i>) = <i>f</i>(<i>x</i>) <i>f</i>(<i>y</i>)</dd>
<dd><i>f</i>(1) = 1.</dd></dl></dd>
<dd>These properties imply that <span class=""nowrap""><i>f</i>(0) = 0</span>, <span class=""nowrap""><i>f</i>(<i>x</i><sup>−1</sup>) = <i>f</i>(<i>x</i>)<sup>−1</sup></span> for <i>x</i> in <i>E</i> with <span class=""nowrap""><i>x</i> ≠ 0</span>, and that <i>f</i> is <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Injective"">injective</a>. Fields, together with these homomorphisms, form a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory"" title=""Category theory"">category</a>. Two fields <i>E</i> and <i>F</i> are called <b>isomorphic</b> if there exists a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Bijective"">bijective</a> homomorphism
<dl><dd><i>f</i>&#160;: <i>E</i> → <i>F</i>.</dd></dl></dd>
<dd>The two fields are then identical for all practical purposes; however, not necessarily in a <i>unique</i> way. See, for example, <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugation"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Complex conjugation"">complex conjugation</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field"" title=""Finite field"">Finite field</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A field with finitely many elements. Aka <b>Galois field</b>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_field"" title=""Ordered field"">Ordered field</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A field with a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order"" title=""Total order"">total order</a> compatible with its operations."

A totally real algebraic number field or a totally imaginary quadratic extension of a totally real field.<sup id=""cite_ref-Sch5_5-0"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-Sch5-5"">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CM-field"" title=""CM-field"">CM-field</a></b> or <b>J-field</b> (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An algebraic number field which is a totally imaginary quadratic extension of a totally real field.<sup id=""cite_ref-6"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-6"">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>"

"<b>Frobenius field</b> (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo_algebraically_closed_field"" title=""Pseudo algebraically closed field"">pseudo algebraically closed field</a> whose <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_Galois_group"" title=""Absolute Galois group"">absolute Galois group</a> has the embedding property.<sup id=""cite_ref-FJ564_8-0"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-FJ564-8"">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl>
<p>Let <i>E</i> / <i>F</i> be a field extension.
</p>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_extension"" title=""Algebraic extension"">Algebraic extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension in which every element of <i>E</i> is algebraic over <i>F</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_extension"" title=""Simple extension"">Simple extension</a></b> (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension which is generated by a single element, called a <b>primitive element</b>, or <b>generating element</b>.<sup id=""cite_ref-9"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-9"">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>   The <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primitive_element_theorem"" title=""Primitive element theorem"">primitive element theorem</a> classifies such extensions.<sup id=""cite_ref-10"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-10"">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_extension"" title=""Normal extension"">Normal extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension that splits a family of polynomials: every root of the minimal polynomial of an element of <i>E</i> over <i>F</i> is also in <i>E</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_extension"" title=""Separable extension"">Separable extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An algebraic extension in which the minimal polynomial of every element of <i>E</i> over <i>F</i> is a <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_polynomial"" title=""Separable polynomial"">separable polynomial</a>, that is, has distinct roots.<sup id=""cite_ref-FJ28_11-0"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-FJ28-11"">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_extension"" title=""Galois extension"">Galois extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A normal, separable field extension."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_extension"" title=""Primary extension"">Primary extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> such that the algebraic closure of <i>F</i> in <i>E</i> is <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purely_inseparable"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Purely inseparable"">purely inseparable</a> over <i>F</i>; equivalently, <i>E</i> is <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_disjoint"" title=""Linearly disjoint"">linearly disjoint</a> from the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_closure"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Separable closure"">separable closure</a> of <i>F</i>.<sup id=""cite_ref-FJ44_12-0"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-FJ44-12"">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purely_transcendental_extension"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Purely transcendental extension"">Purely transcendental extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> in which every element of <i>E</i> not in <i>F</i> is transcendental over <i>F</i>.<sup id=""cite_ref-13"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-13"">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup><sup id=""cite_ref-Isa389_14-0"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-Isa389-14"">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_extension"" title=""Regular extension"">Regular extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> such that <i>E</i> is separable over <i>F</i> and <i>F</i> is algebraically closed in <i>E</i>.<sup id=""cite_ref-FJ44_12-1"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-FJ44-12"">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>"

An extension <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> such that <i>E</i>⊗<sub><i>F</i></sub><i>E</i> is an integral domain.<sup id=""cite_ref-16"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-16"">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b>Totally transcendental extension</b> (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
An extension <i>E</i>/<i>F</i> such that <i>F</i> is algebraically closed in <i>F</i>.<sup id=""cite_ref-Isa389_14-1"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-Isa389-14"">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>"
"<b>Distinguished class</b> (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A class <i>C</i> of field extensions with the three properties<sup id=""cite_ref-17"" class=""reference""><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_field_theory#cite_note-17"">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
<ol><li>If <i>E</i> is a C-extension of <i>F</i> and <i>F</i> is a C-extension of <i>K</i> then <i>E</i> is a C-extension of <i>K</i>.</li>
<li>If <i>E</i> and <i>F</i> are C-extensions of <i>K</i> in a common overfield <i>M</i>, then the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compositum"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Compositum"">compositum</a> <i>EF</i> is a C-extension of <i>K</i>.</li>
<li>If <i>E</i> is a C-extension of <i>F</i> and <i>E</i>&gt;<i>K</i>&gt;<i>F</i> then <i>E</i> is a C-extension of <i>K</i>.</li></ol>"
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_extension"" title=""Galois extension"">Galois extension</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A normal, separable field extension."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_group"" title=""Galois group"">Galois group</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
The <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism_group"" title=""Automorphism group"">automorphism group</a> of a Galois extension. When it is a finite extension, this is a finite group of order equal to the degree of the extension. Galois groups for infinite extensions are <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profinite_group"" title=""Profinite group"">profinite groups</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kummer_theory"" title=""Kummer theory"">Kummer theory</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
The Galois theory of taking <i>n</i>-th roots, given enough <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roots_of_unity"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Roots of unity"">roots of unity</a>. It includes the general theory of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_extension"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Quadratic extension"">quadratic extensions</a>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artin%E2%80%93Schreier_theory"" title=""Artin–Schreier theory"">Artin–Schreier theory</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
Covers an exceptional case of Kummer theory, in characteristic <i>p</i>."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_basis"" title=""Normal basis"">Normal basis</a></b> (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A basis in the vector space sense of <i>L</i> over <i>K</i>, on which the Galois group of <i>L</i> over <i>K</i> acts transitively."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_fields"" title=""Tensor product of fields"">Tensor product of fields</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
A different foundational piece of algebra, including the <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compositum"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Compositum"">compositum</a> operation (<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Join_(mathematics)"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Join (mathematics)"">join</a> of fields)."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_problem_of_Galois_theory"" class=""mw-redirect"" title=""Inverse problem of Galois theory"">Inverse problem of Galois theory</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
Given a group <i>G</i>, find an extension of the rational number or other field with <i>G</i> as Galois group."
"<b><a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_Galois_theory"" title=""Differential Galois theory"">Differential Galois theory</a></b>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
The subject in which symmetry groups of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation"" title=""Differential equation"">differential equations</a> are studied along the lines traditional in Galois theory. This is actually an old idea, and one of the motivations when <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophus_Lie"" title=""Sophus Lie"">Sophus Lie</a> founded the theory of <a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group"" title=""Lie group"">Lie groups</a>. It has not, probably, reached definitive form."
"<a href=""https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grothendieck%27s_Galois_theory"" title=""Grothendieck&#39;s Galois theory"">Grothendieck's Galois theory</a>&#160; (Wikipedia Glossary of Field Theory)"|"
